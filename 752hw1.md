1. Show that the functor $\mathrm{Ext}(-,-)$ is contravariant in the first variable, that is, if $H, H'$ and $G$ are abelian groups, a homomorphism $\alpha: H \rightarrow H'$ induces a homomorphism $\alpha^*: \mathrm{Ext}\left(H', G\right) \rightarrow \mathrm{Ext}(H, G)$.
   
   **Proof**

   The contravariant behavior in the first argument is constructed explicitly using projective resolutions. To define the induced map $\alpha^*: \mathrm{Ext}^n(H', G) \to \mathrm{Ext}^n(H, G)$ for a homomorphism $\alpha: H \to H'$:
   
   Construct projective resolutions $P_{\bullet}$ for $H$ and $P_{\bullet}'$ for $H'$. By the Comparison Theorem for resolutions, the morphism $\alpha: H \to H'$ lifts to a chain map $\alpha_{\bullet}: P_{\bullet} \to P_{\bullet}'$ that is unique up to chain homotopy:
   
   $`\begin{CD}
\dots @>>> P_n @>d_n>> P_{n-1} @>>> \dots @>>> P_0 @>\epsilon>> H @>>> 0 \\
@. @V\alpha_nVV @V\alpha_{n-1}VV @. @V\alpha_0VV @V\alpha VV @. \\
\dots @>>> P_n' @>d_n'>> P_{n-1}' @>>> \dots @>>> P_0' @>\epsilon'>> H' @>>> 0
\end{CD}`$
   
   Drop the augmented $H$ term. Apply the contravariant functor $\mathrm{Hom}(-, G)$ to the resolutions. Because $\mathrm{Hom}$ is contravariant in the first slot, the direction of all arrows—including the lifted maps $\alpha_n$—is reversed: $\mathrm{Hom}(\alpha_n, G): \mathrm{Hom}(P_n', G) \rightarrow \mathrm{Hom}(P_n, G)$. This induces a morphism of cochain complexes $`\alpha^\#: \mathrm{Hom}(P_{\bullet}', G) \rightarrow \mathrm{Hom}(P_{\bullet}, G)`$.

   $`\begin{CD}
\mathrm{Hom}(P_0, G) @>\delta^0>> \mathrm{Hom}(P_1, G) @>\delta^1>> \mathrm{Hom}(P_2, G) @>>> \dots \\
@AAA @AAA @AAA \\
@V\mathrm{Hom}(\alpha_0, G)VV @V\mathrm{Hom}(\alpha_1, G)VV @V\mathrm{Hom}(\alpha_2, G)VV \\
\mathrm{Hom}(P_0', G) @>\delta'^0>> \mathrm{Hom}(P_1', G) @>\delta'^1>> \mathrm{Hom}(P_2', G) @>>> \dots
\end{CD}`$
   
   Passing to cohomology, the induced map on the $n$-th cohomology group is $\alpha^*([g]) = [g \circ \alpha_n]$, where $[g] \in \mathrm{Ext}^n(H', G)$ is the cohomology class of a cocycle $g: P_n' \to G$.

2. Regarding $`\mathbb{Z}_2`$ as a module over the ring $`\mathbb{Z}_4`$, construct a resolution of $`\mathbb{Z}_2`$ by free modules over $`\mathbb{Z}_4`$, and use this to show that $\mathrm{Ext}_{\mathbb{Z}_4}^n(\mathbb{Z}_2, \mathbb{Z}_2)$ is nonzero for all $n$.
   
   **Proof**

   Using the projective resolution
   
   $\dots\xrightarrow{\times2}\mathbb{Z}_4\xrightarrow{\times2}\mathbb{Z}_4\xrightarrow{\bmod 2}\mathbb{Z}_2\to 0$

   Verify exactness at each position.
   
   - At $\mathbb{Z}_2$, the map $\bmod 2$ is surjective by construction, so exactness holds there.
   
   - For $n \geq 1$, consider exactness at the term $\mathbb{Z}_4$ in position $n$. The outgoing differential is $d_n \colon \mathbb{Z}_4 \to \mathbb{Z}_4$, multiplication by 2.
      
      Its kernel is $`\ker(d_n) = \{ x \in \mathbb{Z}_4 \mid 2x = 0 \} = 2\mathbb{Z}_4.`$
      
      The incoming differential is also multiplication by 2, so its image is
      
      $\mathrm{im}(d_{n+1}) = 2\mathbb{Z}_4.$
      
      Thus, $\ker(d_n) = \mathrm{im}(d_{n+1})$ for all $n \geq 1$, establishing exactness at every positive degree.

   - At the term in position 0 ($\mathbb{Z}_4$), the kernel of the augmentation is $`\{ x \in \mathbb{Z}_4 \mid \pi(x) = 0 \in \mathbb{Z}_2 \} = 2\mathbb{Z}_4 = \mathrm{im}(d_1)`$, so the complex is exact at position 0.

   The group $\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbb{Z}_4}(\mathbb{Z}_4, \mathbb{Z}_2)$ consists of the zero map and the non-trivial map sending odd multiples of $1$ to $1$ and even multiples to $0$ (the natural quotient map). Addition of homomorphisms corresponds to addition in $`\mathbb{Z}_2`$, so $`\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbb{Z}_4}(\mathbb{Z}_4, \mathbb{Z}_2) \cong \mathbb{Z}_2`$ via the isomorphism $f \mapsto f(1)$.
   
   The maps in the projective resolution of $\mathbb{Z}_2$ over $\mathbb{Z}_4$ are multiplication by 2, $d_i: \mathbb{Z}_4 \to \mathbb{Z}_4$, $x \mapsto 2x$, for each degree $i \geq 1$.
   
   Applying the functor $\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbb{Z}_4}(-, \mathbb{Z}_2)$ to the projective resolution of $\mathbb{Z}_2$, yielding groups isomorphic to $\mathbb{Z}_2$ in each non-negative degree with differentials induced by precomposition with multiplication by 2. Any $\mathbb{Z}_4$-homomorphism $f: \mathbb{Z}_4 \to \mathbb{Z}_2$ satisfies $f(2x) = 2 \cdot f(x)$. Since the module structure on $\mathbb{Z}_2$ makes the action of 2 trivial (i.e., $2 \cdot y = 0$ for all $y \in \mathbb{Z}_2$), it follows that $2 \cdot f(x) = 0$. Thus, $f \circ (\times 2) = 0$ for every such $f$.

   All differentials therefore vanish, resulting in the cochain complex
$\dots \leftarrow \mathbb{Z}_2 \xleftarrow{\ 0\ } \mathbb{Z}_2 \xleftarrow{\ 0\ } \mathbb{Z}_2 \xleftarrow{\ 0\ } \mathbb{Z}_2 \leftarrow \dots$
with zero maps in every degree.

   Computing homology
   
   $\mathrm{Ext}_{\mathbb{Z}_4}^n(\mathbb{Z}_2, \mathbb{Z}_2) = \ker(0)/\mathrm{im}(0) \cong \mathbb{Z}_2$ for all $n \ge 0$, which is nonzero.

7. For a topological space $X$, let
        
    $\langle,\rangle: C^n(X) \otimes C_n(X) \rightarrow \mathbb{Z}$
    
    be the Kronecker pairing given by $\langle\phi, \sigma\rangle:=\phi(\sigma)$. In terms of this pairing, the coboundary map $\delta: C^n(X) \rightarrow C^{n+1}(X)$ is defined by $\langle\delta(\phi), \sigma\rangle=\langle\phi, \partial \sigma\rangle$ for all $\sigma \in C_{n+1}(X)$. Show that this pairing induces a pairing between cohomology and homology:
    
    $\langle,\rangle: H^n(X ; \mathbb{Z}) \otimes H_n(X ; \mathbb{Z}) \rightarrow \mathbb{Z} .$

    **Proof**

    Define $\langle,\rangle: H^n(X ; \mathbb{Z}) \times H_n(X ; \mathbb{Z}) \rightarrow \mathbb{Z}$ by $\langle [\phi], [\sigma] \rangle = \phi(\sigma)$.
    This map is well-defined, and independent of the choice of representatives:
     - If $[\phi]=0$, then $\phi = \delta\psi$. For any cycle $\sigma$, $\langle [\phi], [\sigma] \rangle = \phi(\sigma) = (\delta\psi)(\sigma) = \psi(\partial\sigma) = \psi(0) = 0$.
     - If $[\sigma]=0$, then $\sigma = \partial\tau$. For any cocycle $\phi$, $\langle [\phi], [\sigma] \rangle = \phi(\sigma) = \phi(\partial\tau) = (\delta\phi)(\tau) = 0(\tau) = 0$.
    
    This map is also bilinear, so it induces a map $\langle,\rangle: H^n(X ; \mathbb{Z}) \otimes H_n(X ; \mathbb{Z}) \rightarrow \mathbb{Z}$.
   
    Non-degeneracy:
    Take a basis for $C_n(X)$ and consider the dual basis in $C^n(X)$.
